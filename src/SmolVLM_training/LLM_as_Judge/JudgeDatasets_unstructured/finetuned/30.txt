 The bar chart illustrates the leading causes of death among U.S. millennials aged 25 to 29 in 2017, by percentage of total deaths.

The most prominent cause of death among this age group was accidents, accounting for 45% of total deaths. This was followed by suicides, which contributed 14.4%. Homicide ranked third, with 10.9% of deaths attributed to this cause. Malignant neoplasms accounted for 4.6% of deaths, while diseases of the heart contributed 4.5%.

Overall, accidents and suicides were the leading causes of death among U.S. millennials aged 25 to 29 in 2017, with a combined percentage of 60%. Malignant neoplasms and diseases of the heart were the next most common causes, accounting for 10.9% and 4.5% of deaths, respectively. The remaining causes, including homicide and other unspecified causes, contributed a smaller percentage to the total.

This data highlights the significant impact of accidents and suicides on the mortality rate among U.S. millennials aged 25 to 29 in 2017. The data also indicates that diseases of the heart and malignant neoplasms were relatively less prevalent compared to accidents and suicides, underscoring the importance of addressing these causes for public health interventions.
